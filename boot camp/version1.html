A "Bicameral" Legislature

The Legislative Branch is made up of TWO houses. This is called a bicameral system. The Founding Fathers set it up this way during the Great Compromise to balance power.

House of Representatives

  • Members: 435
  • Representation: Based on state population (Bigger states = more reps)
  • Term Length: 2 years
  • Minimum Age: 25 years old
  • Vibe: Meant to be closer to the people and their daily needs.

The Senate

  • Members: 100
  • Representation: Equal! Exactly 2 per state regardless of size.
  • Term Length: 6 years
  • Minimum Age: 30 years old
  • Vibe: Meant to be a more stable, deliberative body.

The Powers of Congress

Article I, Section 8 of the Constitution gives you your superpowers. These are called expressed powers.

(Click the cards to reveal the powers!)

Money Power

Congress can levy taxes, borrow money, and coin/print money.

War Power

Only Congress has the power to declare war and raise armies/navies.

Commerce Power

Congress regulates trade (commerce) between states and with other countries.

Postal Power

Congress establishes the Post Office and postal routes.

Impeachment

Congress can charge and remove federal officials (like the President) for bad behavior.

The "Elastic" Clause

Congress can make any laws that are "necessary and proper" to carry out their other powers!

How a Bill Becomes a Law

It is a long, difficult obstacle course for an idea (a "bill") to actually become a law. Follow the steps below!

1

The Idea & Introduction

Anyone can have an idea, but only a member of Congress can introduce a bill in the House or Senate.

2

Committee Action

The bill goes to a smaller group of experts (a committee). They debate it, change it, or kill it. Most bills die here!

3

Floor Vote

If it survives committee, the whole House or Senate debates and votes on it. It needs a majority (over 50%) to pass.

4

The Other Chamber

If the House passes it, it goes to the Senate (and vice versa). It must pass BOTH houses in the exact same form.

5

The President's Desk

The President can sign it (it becomes law!) or veto it (reject it).
Note: Congress can override a veto with a 2/3 vote, but it's very hard!
