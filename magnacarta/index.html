🎓 How Did Constitutional Government Develop in Great Britain?

Lesson Purpose

Constitutional government developed in Great Britain over a period of many centuries. In this lesson, you learn how the monarchy came to share power with the nobles. You will study some documents that limited the power of the British government. This study will help you better understand our ideas about limited government.

Lesson Objectives

  • Describe the struggles for power between the English monarch and Parliament.
  • Explain how these struggles led to a system of separated powers and representative government.
  • Describe some of the important constitutional documents in British history that influenced the writing of our constitution.

🏰 What was the feudal system?

After 1066, a system called **feudalism** was established in England. This was a system of social, economic, and political organization based on the control of land. Under feudalism, the people in England belonged to one of three social groups:

Royalty

The king and his family. The king was the ultimate ruler and owned all the land.

Nobility

Lords and ladies who held titles. The king granted land (fiefs) to nobles (vassals) in exchange for loyalty and military service.

Common People

Included knights, soldiers, merchants, craftsmen, and peasants (or serfs). They worked the land for the nobles.

Feudalism and Contracts

The feudal system was important for the development of constitutional government because it was based on a system of agreements or contracts. The relationship between the nobles and the king depended on both sides fulfilling their obligations. This introduced the idea of a **contract between the ruler and the ruled**.

⚖️ Why did the American colonists have the rights of Englishmen?

At the time of the American Revolution, colonists in Great Britain's North American colonies were considered **subjects** of the British monarchy. As subjects, they were believed to have all the rights that all subjects of the English king had.

These core beliefs, which had developed over centuries, included:

  • The right to a **trial by jury**.
  • Security from **unlawful entry** into one's home.
  • **No taxation without consent**.

These "rights of Englishmen" were based on common law and key documents. **Parliament** is the legislative body of British government, and its long history of struggle with the monarchy helped establish these rights.

📜 Why is the Magna Carta an important document?

In 1215, English nobles forced King John to sign an agreement, the **Magna Carta** (or "Great Charter"). This was perhaps the most important early example of a written statement of law limiting the power of a ruler.

The Magna Carta contains two important ideas that influenced the Founders:

1. Government as a Contract

The document was a contract between the king and the nobility. It established the idea that if either side breaks the contract, that contract is no longer valid. Government is based on an agreement between the ruler and the people.

2. The Rule of Law

Both government and the governed must obey the law. This is called the **rule of law**. The law limits the powers of government. The king could not take away property or imprison people without following due-process procedures and rules.

🏛️ Why was the creation of Parliament important?

In 1258, nobles forced the king to create an advisory council. This council eventually evolved into the English **Parliament**, the legislative branch of the government. Parliament grew to have two houses:

  • The House of Lords: Represented the nobles.
  • The House of Commons: Represented people (who owned large amounts of land but were not nobles) from different regions.

For centuries, the monarchy, nobility, and commons struggled for power. This struggle eventually led to a civil war in the 1600s. By 1688, a power shift had occurred, giving Parliament more power than the monarch.

✍️ What was the Petition of Right (1628)?

In 1628, King Charles I was forced to sign the **Petition of Right**. This document was a result of Parliament's pressure to stop the king from abusing his power.

The Petition of Right strengthened the idea that English subjects had certain rights that the government could not violate. It specifically stated that the king could not:

  • Raise taxes without the consent of Parliament.
  • House soldiers in the homes of the people (quartering).
  • Imprison people without a just cause.

👑 What was the English Bill of Rights (1689)?

In 1689, Parliament passed the **English Bill of Rights**. This law further limited the powers of the monarch and gave more power to Parliament. By this time, the British government was much more limited in what it could do.

The English Bill of Rights stated that the monarch was no longer allowed to:

  • Collect taxes without the consent of Parliament.
  • Interfere with the right to free speech and debate in Parliament.
  • Maintain an army in peacetime.
  • Prevent Protestants from having arms for their defense.
  • Require excessive bail or administer cruel punishment.
  • Declare that laws made by Parliament should not be obeyed.

🤔 Critical Thinking: Examining the Magna Carta

Here are some rights from the Magna Carta. Click on each one to see what it means and why it's important.

"For a trivial [minor] offence, a free man shall be fined only in proportion to the degree of his offence, and for a serious offence correspondingly."

What it means: The punishment should fit the crime. A small crime gets a small punishment, and a big crime gets a big punishment.

Why it's important: This prevents the king or a judge from assigning an unfairly harsh punishment, like taking all of someone's property for a minor mistake. It's the basis for the 8th Amendment in the U.S. Constitution, which protects against "cruel and unusual punishments."

"No free man shall be taken, or imprisoned... or in any way harmed... save by the lawful judgment of his peers or by the law of the land."

What it means: People cannot be arrested, jailed, or have their property taken away without a fair legal process. This includes a "judgment of his peers" (a trial by jury) or following established rules ("the law of the land").

Why it's important: This is the foundation of **due process of law**. It means the government must follow its own rules and cannot punish people arbitrarily. This directly influenced the 5th, 6th, and 7th Amendments of the U.S. Constitution.

"No [official]... shall take the corn [grain] or other chattels [personal property] of anyone... unless he gives money for them."

What it means: The government cannot take a person's private property without paying for it.

Why it's important: This establishes the right to property. It limits the government's power, preventing officials from simply seizing whatever they want from citizens. This is the basis for the "takings clause" in the 5th Amendment of the U.S. Constitution (eminent domain).

"To no one will we sell, to no one deny or delay, right of justice."

What it means: Everyone has a right to a fair and timely trial. The legal system should not be biased by bribes ("sell justice") or withheld from people ("deny or delay justice").

Why it's important: This guarantees **equal justice under the law** and the right to a speedy trial. It ensures that justice isn't just for the rich or powerful.
